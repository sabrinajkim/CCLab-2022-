Sword Dance 검무 (geommu)

Sword dance (geommu) also known as kalchum, is a militaristic yet graceful dance that roots back to a tale of a boy named Hwangchang who had incredible prowess for dancing with the sword, so much so that the king of a rival kingdom invited him to come and perform in his court. The boy ended up slaying the king, and was killed himself afterwards. His home kingdom honored him, however, by dancing with swords and masks in his honor.

Three Drum 삼고무 (samgomu)

Three drum also known as sam buk (삼북) is a traditional korean dance where each performer is surounded by three drums. Traditionally performed at the royal palace, samgomu is known to display the most dynamic and grandiose dance movements. Starting with a slow beat that gradually turns into a faster and more powerful rythm.

Fan Dance 부채춤 (buchaechum)

Fan dance is known for its beautiful colors and movements. Its origins date back to the Joseon Dynasty(1392-1910). The movements of the dance evoke natural phenomena, from the color and fringes of the peony-colored fans, to the many formations that recall blooming trees, flowers, and natural waves. This choreography and costuming are closely related to the shamanic religious tradition of the Korean spiritual beliefs.

Scarf Dance 화관무 (hwagwanmu)

Hwa-gwan-mu also known as Flower-Crown-Dance is one of the oldest forms of court dances in Korea. Dancers are elegantly dressed in full court attire and wear crowns beautifully ornamented with beads of five different colors. Traditionally, the dancers were members of the royal art agencies and performed at banquets for the royal family and foreign envoys.

Janggu 장구첨 (janggu chum)

Janggu, a national percussion instrument, has been used since Koguryo Kingdom (B.C. 277-A.D. 668). Originally, the Janggu dance was a part of peasant dance and song-and-dance play. It was developed into an independent item like solo dance and group dance with rich dance movements. This dance is characterized by zestful rhythmic movements and strong national emotion.
